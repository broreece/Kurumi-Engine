Coupling

Coupling refers to the degree to which objects depend on one another.
When systems are tightly coupled, components rely heavily on specific implementations, making the codebase harder to 
test, maintain, and extend.

Our goal is to maintain a loosely coupled architecture, where components interact through well-defined interfaces and
abstractions. However, complete decoupling is not always practical or desirable.

Acceptable Cases for Coupling
Certain scenarios inherently require tighter coupling:
- Runtime-owned objects
- Objects that are not data-driven (e.g. not loaded from JSON/YAML) and are directly managed by other objects (e.g. 
    Entity Modifiers, Controllers).
- State-driven runtime logic
- Systems that operate on live models and require direct access to both definitions and runtime data.
- Service dependencies on runtime data
- Services that must interact with concrete runtime objects to perform their responsibilities.

Benefits of Decoupling
Maintaining a decoupled system provides several key advantages:
- Improved testability
- Individual components can be tested in isolation without complex setup.
- Reduced dependencies
- Less reliance on imports and concrete implementations simplifies the codebase.
- Clear separation of concerns
- Aligns with SOLID principles, ensuring each class has a focused responsibility.
- Greater flexibility
- Systems can evolve, be replaced, or extended with minimal impact on existing code.
